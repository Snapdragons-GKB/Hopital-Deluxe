What we used, how we flowed

Hopital was created using fullstack Django with PSQL. Two of us had no previous Python experience (none) before starting Hopital, and it was a bit of a risk to take with a one-week project. Nevertheless, we dove into the work and got it done. Bella handled front end integration of our data, Kate handled styling, and Gavin handled the backend of our project. We somewhat naively managed to run into every wall imaginable along the way, so we will discuss several increasingly hilarious failures we ran into. Almost all of them were Gavin's fault, so we'll let him take the lead on this one.


A common antipattern in our development process with Django was that it liked to defy gravity. We always planned to have authentication (cough *HIPAA* cough), and we started with Django's built-in authentication system, which is quite approachable. However, we also planned to have multiple user logins. We began building them out, but nothing was working. I started chopping away at necessary parts that were innate to the base authentication system, and it kept working. I was baffled. After some painful refactoring, we had multiple user logins. We were so proud of ourselves. We were so wrong. We had no idea what we were doing.


I then began to extend our user model creations. Again, extension wasn't working. Again, I started chopping away, and it again kept working. I then dove into abstract user model extension, and after more lines and a couple extra data tables, we had working user models.


The cream of the crop, however, lay in the tall grass of login pathing for custom user models. After we had custom user models for the three classes, our login system took a nosedive. I was pretty baffled. I ended up having to create a custom backend for login, and everything was great, until it wasn't working either. A couple days prior, I had noticed that our passwords, per Django standard, were stored hashed, which is common and reasonable. What I didn't realized until four in the morning the day of presentation was, well, I'll leave it to a limerick I wrote.


There once was a boy imprecise

With two passwords that weren't very nice

They riddled him with words

Til he realized the absurd

My god, I hashed them both twice


I had been comparing my stored passwords to the correctly hashed input, but I had actually been comparing my hashed stored hashed passwords to our hashed input. Ouch. Understanding totients, implementing hashes, not the same thing.


It was pretty smooth sailing from there. It looked a bit rough in our presentation, but we had a ton of depth in the structural work we did behind the scenes. We were able to get a lot of the functionality we wanted in, and it was a vigorous learning experience to say the least.
